Project Overview

Precise light control for next-generation chip manufacturing

ASML — the world's leading manufacturer of Extreme Ultraviolet (EUV) photolithography machines — challenged our team to design a lens-tilting mechanism capable of directing a focal point with sub-millimeter precision while remaining self-correcting in the face of vibration and thermal change.

Our solution uses three Actuonix P8-ST linear stepper actuators arranged at 120° intervals beneath a 100 mm lens, paired with a radially compliant flexure mount that protects the lens from stress during thermal expansion. An onboard gyroscope and accelerometer enable real-time self-adjustment.

The core principle — accurate optical tilting with passive stress relief — has broad applications in medical imaging, fiber optics, laser cutting, and high-resolution microscopy.

Design Process

Three concepts. One winner.

We evaluated three actuation concepts against eleven weighted engineering criteria before converging on linear actuation.

01

Gyroscope-Shaped

Three concentric rings providing independent rotation on two axes. High accuracy, but complex wiring and difficult assembly made integration challenging.

02

Tilt-Track & Rotator

Separates tilt and pitch into distinct motions, combining them via a single monodirectional tilt + 180° rotation. Eliminated due to space and torque constraints.

03

Linear Actuation ✓

Three Actuonix P8-ST linear stepper actuators at 120° intervals beneath the lens. Highest scores in ease of assembly, wiring, and manufacturability. Final score: 149/175.

Architecture

Four integrated subsystems

SUBSYSTEM 01

Optic Mount

A radially compliant mechanism using three cantilever-style AISI 1020 flexures at 120° spacing. Allows the 100 mm lens to expand thermally in the XY plane while staying rigid in Z. Stiffness ratio achieved: 1728:1 (requirement: 1000:1).

SUBSYSTEM 02

Base & Actuation

Three Actuonix P8-ST linear stepper actuators provide 0.01 mm step resolution and 25 mm stroke. Ball-and-socket joints connect each actuator to the mounting ring, converting vertical displacement into precise lens tilt.

SUBSYSTEM 03

Inverse Kinematics & Control

A parallel manipulator model translates desired focal point coordinates (x, y) into actuator positions using trigonometric projection and vector algebra. Motor steps are mapped directly to vertical displacement.

SUBSYSTEM 04

Testing Rig

A three-legged rigid tripod with a central ring platform, supporting two test configurations: lens hanging below (Config 1) or resting above (Config 2), enabling flexible alignment and clearance testing.

Analysis & Results

Validated by simulation and analysis

FEA simulations in SolidWorks confirmed the design meets all thermal, fatigue, and stiffness requirements.

Thermal Expansion of Lens

With a 15°C temperature increase applied, the lens expands radially by just 0.00128 mm (1.28 µm). Peak stress of 8.5 MPa — well below the glass yield strength of 35 MPa — was observed at flexure contact points.

Max stress: 8.5 MPa ✓

Flexure Stress & Factor of Safety

Under the prescribed thermal displacement, the flexures reach a maximum Von Mises stress of only 6.1 MPa against a yield strength of 351.6 MPa — yielding a factor of safety of 57.6.

FOS: 57.6 ✓

Fatigue Life of Flexures

Using the Stress-Life method (Shigley's 11th Ed.), the calculated fatigue life of the AISI 1020 flexures under repeated thermal cycling was found to be 2.83 × 10²⁷ cycles — effectively infinite.

Fatigue life: Infinite ✓

Stiffness Ratio

XY stiffness (flexure compliance) was calculated at 2.28 × 10⁵ N/m. Z-direction stiffness combining flexure and mounting ring lip reached 3.94 × 10⁸ N/m — a ratio of 1728:1, exceeding the 1000:1 requirement.

Ratio: 1728:1 (req: 1000:1) ✓
